# Chapter 1: Instructions for Practical Living, Part 1 傳習錄上

#### Xu Ai's Preface

On the various doctrines of *gewu* — the "investigation of things" — in the *Great Learning*, the Master takes the old text as the correct one. That is the very text the earlier Confucians[^chuanxi-1] called corrupted. When I first heard this, I was startled; then I doubted; then I drove my mind to exhaustion, weaving objections together and apart, in order to put them before him. Only then did I come to know that the Master's teaching has the certainty of water's cold and fire's heat — that even after a hundred generations, when at last a sage arises again, this teaching will meet his scrutiny without confusion. The Master's brilliance is the gift of Heaven, and yet he is mild and at ease, without affectation. Those who saw him in his youth — bold and untamed, given to literary indulgence, ranging in and out of the Two Schools[^chuanxi-2] — when they suddenly hear his teaching now take him for a man fond of novelty for its own sake, and they refuse to look closely. They do not know that during the Master's three years among the southern peoples,[^chuanxi-3] in hardship and in cultivated stillness, his discipline of *discrimination and unity*[^chuanxi-4] had already lifted him into the domain of sages, and that he returned to us, pure and whole, in the Great Mean and Utmost Rectitude.

I have warmed myself at his door morning and evening. I see that the closer I come, the higher he rises; the rougher he seems on the surface, the finer his texture beneath; the nearer one stands, the more inexhaustible the depth. More than ten years, and I have still not glimpsed his outer hedge. Some gentlemen of the world have met the Master only once, or have not even heard him cough; some come already aggrieved or contemptuous, and yet, from a chance street-corner exchange or some piece of hearsay, presume to take his measure. How could that ever succeed? Even those who study under him often grasp one point and lose two — like buyers who fixate on a horse's coat and color and miss that they are looking at a thousand-mile racer. So I have set down what I have heard, day after day, and circulated it privately among fellow students, asking them to weigh and correct it together with me, in the hope that we shall not betray the Master's teaching.

— *Recorded by his student, Xu Ai.*

[^chuanxi-1]: That is, the Cheng-Zhu school: the Cheng brothers (Cheng Hao, 1032–1085, and Cheng Yi, 1033–1107) and especially Zhu Xi (1130–1200), whose recension of the *Great Learning* rearranged the chapters and added an interpolated commentary on *gewu*. Wang's polemic targets that recension throughout.
[^chuanxi-2]: Buddhism and Daoism, conventionally so named relative to the Confucian.
[^chuanxi-3]: Wang Yangming's exile (1508–1510) to Longchang, in what is now Guizhou — then a remote frontier among non-Han peoples. The classical phrase echoes *Analects* 9.13, where Confucius muses on going to live among the *yi*.
[^chuanxi-4]: 惟精惟一 *wéi jīng wéi yī*, "with utmost discrimination, with utmost unity" — the formula in the *Documents*, "Counsels of Yu the Great," that Wang treats throughout this work as the kernel of the sagely method.


**1.** I asked: "On the phrase *zai qin min* — 'in loving the people' — in the *Great Learning*, Master Zhu argues we should read it as *xin min*, 'renewing the people,' and the later passage *zuo xin min*, 'stirring up a renewed people,' does seem to support him. The Master holds with the old text and reads *qin min*. Is there evidence on the other side as well?"

The Master said: "*Zuo xin min* — there the *xin* refers to a *self-renewed* people. That is not the same as the *xin* in the program-statement *zai xin min*. How could it count as evidence? Set against *qin*, the right pairing word is *zuo*; but that does not give *qin* the meaning *xin*. And below, in the sections on 'governing the state' and 'pacifying the world,' the text never elaborates *xin* further. When it says, *the gentleman makes worthy those who are worthy and treats his kin as kin; the petty man takes pleasure in his pleasures and profits by his profits*; or *as one cares for an infant*; or *the people's likes he likes, the people's dislikes he dislikes — this is to be called the people's father and mother* — all of this carries the sense of *qin*, not *xin*. *Qin min* is just what Mencius means by *treating one's kin as kin and being humane to the people*. To make them *qin* is to make them *ren* [humane]. When the people were estranged, Shun appointed Xie as Minister of Education and had him reverently spread the five teachings — that was the means of making them *qin*. In the *Canon of Yao*, 'making bright the lofty virtue' is *ming ming de*, illuminating bright virtue; and the sequence from 'making kin of the nine clans' down to 'pacifying and harmonizing the myriad states' is *qin min* — which is precisely *ming ming de* applied to the world. Likewise, when Confucius said *cultivating oneself in order to bring peace to the people*, 'cultivating oneself' is *ming ming de*, and 'bringing peace to the people' is *qin min*. To say *qin min* is to comprehend both teaching and nourishing; to say *xin min* leans to one side."

**2.** I asked: "On *Knowing where to rest, one is settled* — Master Zhu holds that 'each thing and affair has its own settled principle.' This seems to contradict the Master's view."

The Master said: "To seek the supreme good in things and affairs is to make rightness external. The supreme good is the original substance of the mind: it is just *ming ming de* carried through to its 'utmost discrimination, utmost unity.' Yet this never separates from things and affairs. The original commentary on the text has it: *fully realizing the extreme of Heaven's principle, with not a thread of human selfish desire*. That hits it."

**3.** I asked: "If the supreme good is sought only in the mind, I worry one cannot exhaust the principles of every affair under Heaven."

The Master said: "The mind *is* principle. Could there be, anywhere under Heaven, an affair outside the mind, or a principle outside the mind?"

I said: "But take serving one's father with filiality, serving one's lord with loyalty, dealing with friends in good faith, governing the people humanely — there are so many principles bound up in these. Surely one cannot fail to look into them?"

The Master sighed: "This way of speaking has clouded thought for too long; it cannot be cleared with a single sentence. But to take what you ask: if, in order to serve your father, you go to your father to seek the principle of filiality; if, to serve your lord, you go to your lord to seek the principle of loyalty; if, to deal with friends or govern the people, you go to friends and to the people to seek the principles of good faith and humaneness — all these principles are right here, in this mind. The mind is principle. When this mind is unobstructed by selfish desire, it *is* Heaven's principle, and there is nothing to add from outside. With a mind purely Heaven's principle, when it issues forth in serving one's father, it is filiality; when it issues forth in serving one's lord, it is loyalty; when it issues forth in friendship or in governance, it is good faith and humaneness. The whole work is in this mind: clearing away selfish desire and preserving Heaven's principle."

I said: "Hearing the Master say this, I already feel something opening. But the old way of speaking still tangles me up. Take serving one's father — there are many specifics: keeping him warm in winter, cool in summer, settling him at night, greeting him at dawn. Do we still have to inquire into these?"

The Master said: "Of course we inquire. The point is that there is one head to the matter, and the inquiry is by way of clearing away selfish desire and preserving Heaven's principle in this mind. To inquire how to keep him warm in winter is just to seek to fulfill the filiality of this mind, and to guard against any thread of selfish desire mixing in. To inquire how to keep him cool in summer is again just to fulfill the filiality of this mind. The whole inquiry is for the sake of this mind. If this mind is without selfish desire and is purely Heaven's principle — a mind sincerely filial to its parents — then in winter it will of itself think of the parents' cold and seek the means of warming them; in summer it will of itself think of the parents' heat and seek the means of cooling them. These specifics all issue out of the sincerely filial mind. The sincerely filial mind must come first, and the specifics will follow. As with a tree: the sincerely filial mind is the root, the particulars are the branches and leaves. There must first be the root, and then there are branches and leaves; one does not first plant out branches and leaves and afterwards go looking for a root. The *Record of Rites* says: *The filial son who has deep love must have a harmonious air; he who has a harmonious air must have a glad face; he who has a glad face must have a yielding bearing*. Once there is deep love at the root, the rest comes of itself."

**4.** Zheng Chaoshuo asked: "Surely the supreme good must also be sought from things and affairs?"

The Master said: "The supreme good is just this mind made purely Heaven's principle to its utmost. What more is there to seek from things and affairs? Try giving me a few examples."

Chaoshuo said: "Take serving one's parent. How is one to manage the warming and the cooling? How is one to attend to nourishment in the right way? One needs to find the proper measure — and that is the supreme good. So there must be the work of *learning, inquiring, thinking, discerning*."

The Master said: "If it were only a question of warming and cooling, of how to nourish properly, those particulars could be talked through in a day or two — what need is there of *learning, inquiring, thinking, discerning*? The point is that, *while* one warms or cools, this mind must be purely Heaven's principle to its utmost; *while* one attends to nourishment, this mind must be purely Heaven's principle to its utmost. Without the work of learning, inquiring, thinking, discerning — applied to *that* — even the smallest deviation will travel a thousand miles. This is why even sages still received the teaching of 'discrimination and unity.' If one calls 'getting the particulars right' the supreme good, then an actor who has mastered the warming, cooling, and nourishing routines on stage might be called supremely good as well." I had a fresh insight that day.

**5.** I had not understood the Master's teaching of *the unity of knowing and acting*, and had argued back and forth with my friends Zongxian and Weixian without resolution. I put it to the Master.

The Master said: "Give me an example."

I said: "Take people today. Many of them know that a son ought to be filial and a younger brother ought to be deferential, and yet they are unable to be filial, unable to be deferential. So knowing and acting are clearly two separate things."

The Master said: "Such people have already been cut off from themselves by selfish desire — that is not the original substance of knowing and acting. There has never been one who *truly knows* and does not act. To know and not to act is simply not yet to know. When the sages and worthies taught knowing and acting, they meant precisely to restore that original substance — not to leave you content with the broken thing as it is. So the *Great Learning* shows real knowing-and-acting plainly: *as one loves a beautiful color, as one hates a foul stench*. Seeing the beautiful color is knowing; *loving* it is acting. The instant one sees the color one already loves it — one does not first see and then set up a separate intention to love. Smelling the foul stench is knowing; hating it is acting. The instant one smells it one already hates it — one does not first smell and then set up a separate intention to hate. A person whose nose is stopped up will not strongly hate even a foul stench held under it: he simply has not yet *known* the stench. Or take saying 'so-and-so knows filiality, so-and-so knows fraternal deference.' That person must already have *acted* with filiality, *acted* with deference, before we can say he knows them. We do not call a person who can recite some sentences about filiality and deference one who *knows* filiality and deference. Or take 'knowing pain': you must already have hurt before you know pain. Knowing cold — you must already have been cold. Knowing hunger — you must already have been hungry. How can knowing and acting be separated at all? *That* is the original substance of knowing and acting, before any private intention has cut it apart. The sages taught people in just this way — and only this counts as knowing. Anything less is not yet to know. What an urgent and concrete piece of work this is! Why should we cling so tightly to making knowing and acting two? What does my saying they are one come to? If you do not understand the underlying point, no amount of saying 'one' or 'two' will help."

I said: "When the ancients spoke of knowing and acting as two, they meant to give us a clearer view — one strand for the work of knowing, one strand for the work of acting — so that the work has somewhere to land."

The Master said: "But that misses the ancients' point. I have always said: knowing is the *intent* of acting; acting is the *work* of knowing. Knowing is the *beginning* of acting; acting is the *completion* of knowing. Once you understand, then to say merely *knowing* already contains the acting; to say merely *acting* already contains the knowing. The reason the ancients spoke of *both* a knowing and an acting was that there is one kind of person in the world who muddles along doing whatever, with no thought, no self-examination, blundering through life — for such a person you must speak of *knowing* before they will act rightly; and there is another kind of person, drifting in mid-air, only thinking and never bringing himself down to put a single thing into practice — for such a person you must speak of *acting* before he will *truly* know. This was the ancients' makeshift remedy for opposite ailments. If you grasp the point, a single word would suffice. But people today take knowing and acting as two separate businesses; they say one must first know completely and then act, and so 'I will discuss and study now in order to do the work of knowing, and only when I really know will I take up the work of acting.' Hence to the end of their lives they do not act — and to the end of their lives they do not know. This is no small disorder, and it has been with us a long time. To say *the unity of knowing and acting* is to administer the medicine to the illness. I am not making something up: the original substance of knowing and acting is just this. Once you grasp the point, even saying 'two' does no harm — they are still one. If you have not grasped the point, then even saying 'one' will accomplish nothing. It is just talk."

**6.** I asked: "Yesterday, hearing the Master's teaching of *resting in the supreme good*, I felt the work had a place to land. But it still does not square with Master Zhu's *gewu* doctrine when I think it over."

The Master said: "*Gewu* is the work of *resting in the supreme good*. Once you know the supreme good, you know *gewu*."

I said: "I tried, yesterday, to extend the Master's teaching to *gewu* and saw the broad outline. But Master Zhu's account, with its appeal to the *Documents*' 'discrimination and unity,' the *Analects*' 'broad and restrained,' and the *Mencius*'s 'fully realizing the mind and knowing nature' — these all seem to support him, and I cannot quite let go."

The Master said: "Zixia trusted the sages firmly; Zengzi turned back upon himself. Trusting firmly is fine, but turning back upon oneself is more incisive. If your own mind cannot make peace with a teaching, how can you lazily defer to received opinion and not seek the right? Even Master Zhu, with his reverence for Master Cheng, did not blindly accept passages where his own mind could not make peace. *Discrimination and unity*, *broad and restrained*, *fully realizing the mind* — all of these in fact accord exactly with what I am saying; you simply have not thought it through. Master Zhu's account of *gewu* unavoidably forces the texts together, and is not their original meaning. *Discrimination* is the work for *unity*; *broad* is the work for *restrained*. Once Yueren grasped *the unity of knowing and acting*, this becomes clear in a sentence. *Fully realizing the mind, knowing nature, knowing Heaven* belongs to those who *know by birth and act with ease*; *preserving the mind, nourishing nature, serving Heaven* belongs to those who *know by learning and act for benefit*; *short life and long do not divide the heart, cultivate oneself and await it* belongs to those who *know by struggle and act by effort*.[^chuanxi-5] Master Zhu's *gewu* doctrine inverts this — taking *fully realizing the mind, knowing nature* as 'things investigated, knowledge fully reached,' so that beginners are made to start with what only those who know by birth can do. How is that workable?"

[^chuanxi-5]: The three rungs come from *Mencius* 7A.1. Wang's gloss links each to the three modes of moral knowing in *Zhongyong* 20.

I asked: "Why does *fully realizing the mind, knowing nature* belong to *knowing by birth, acting with ease*?"

The Master said: "Nature is the substance of the mind; Heaven is the source of nature. To fully realize the mind is just to fully realize one's nature. *Only the utmost sincerity of the world is able to fully realize his own nature, and to know Heaven and Earth's transforming and nourishing.* 'Preserving the mind' implies that the mind has not yet been fully realized. To *know* Heaven, in the sense of *fully realizing the mind, knowing nature, knowing Heaven*, is like 'governor' and 'magistrate' — what is your own to manage, where you are already one with Heaven. To *serve* Heaven, in the sense of *preserving the mind, nourishing nature, serving Heaven*, is like a son serving his father, a minister serving his lord — one must be reverent and faithful, only then is one without lapse — and one is still two with Heaven. That is precisely the difference between sage and worthy. As for *short life and long do not divide the heart, cultivate oneself and await it* — that is teaching the learner to single-mindedly do good and not to let happiness or hardship, long life or short, sway the doing-good mind. One simply *cultivates oneself and awaits the mandate*. One sees that hardship and ease, long life and short, lie in the mandate, and resolves not to let one's own heart be moved by such things. *Serving Heaven*, while it leaves one as two with Heaven, is at least already aware of Heaven before one's face. *Awaiting the mandate* is not yet to have seen the face — one is still here, on the way, waiting. That is the beginner's first taking up of resolve, with effort and struggle behind it. As things now stand, the order has been inverted, and beginners have nowhere to set their hands."

I said: "Yesterday's lesson hinted at this. Now the picture clears. Last evening I was thinking that the *wu*, 'thing,' in *gewu* is just the same as the *shi*, 'affair' — and both are spoken of in terms of the mind."

The Master said: "Just so. The body's master is the mind; what the mind sends forth is *intent*; the original substance of intent is *knowing*; what intent rests upon is *thing*. If the intent rests on serving one's father, then serving one's father is one *thing*. If on serving one's lord, then serving one's lord is one *thing*. If on humaneness toward the people and care for things, then humaneness toward the people and care for things is one *thing*. If on seeing, hearing, speaking, moving, then those are one *thing*. So I say: *no principle outside the mind, no thing outside the mind*. The *Doctrine of the Mean* says, *Without sincerity there are no things*. The *Great Learning*'s work of *illuminating bright virtue* is just *making one's intent sincere*. And the work of making intent sincere is just *gewu*."

The Master said again: "*Gewu* — the *ge* is as in the *Mencius*'s *correcting* the heart of the great man[^chuanxi-6] — to remove from one's heart what is not upright, in order to make whole what is upright in its original substance. Wherever intent rests, you remove what is not upright in order to fulfill what is upright. Then there is no time and no place where one is not preserving Heaven's principle — and that is *fathoming principle*. Heaven's principle just *is* bright virtue; fathoming principle just *is* illuminating bright virtue."

[^chuanxi-6]: *Mencius* 4A.20: 大人格君心之非, "the great man corrects what is not upright in his ruler's heart."

The Master said again: "Knowing is the original substance of the mind, and the mind naturally knows: seeing one's father, it naturally knows filiality; seeing one's elder brother, it naturally knows fraternal deference; seeing a child about to fall into a well, it naturally knows alarm and pity. *That* is innate knowing — *liangzhi* — and there is no need to seek it outside. If, when innate knowing issues forth, no selfish intent obstructs it, then *one fills out one's heart of pity, and humaneness becomes inexhaustible*. But the ordinary person cannot help having selfish intentions in the way, and so requires the work of *extending knowing and rectifying things* in order to overcome the selfish and restore the principle. When the innate knowing of the mind no longer suffers obstruction, and may fill out and flow forth — that is *extending its knowing*. With knowing extended, intent is sincere."

**7.** I asked: "The Master takes *broad in literary culture* as the work for *restrained by ritual*. I cannot follow this. May I have a hint?"

The Master said: "*Ritual* (*li*) is just *principle* (*li*).[^chuanxi-7] Where principle issues forth visibly, we call it *culture*; where culture is hidden and unseen, we call it *principle*. They are one thing. To be *restrained by ritual* is just to make this mind purely a single Heaven's principle. To make this mind purely Heaven's principle, one must work where principle issues forth. When it issues forth in serving one's father, work in serving one's father to preserve this Heaven's principle. When it issues forth in serving one's lord, work there. In wealth and honor or in poverty and want, work there. In hardship or among foreign tribes, work there. Down to acting and pausing, speaking and falling silent — there is nowhere it does not appear. Wherever it appears, work there to preserve Heaven's principle. *That* is breadth in culture, and *that* is the work of being restrained by ritual. *Broad in culture* is *utmost discrimination*; *restrained by ritual* is *utmost unity*."

[^chuanxi-7]: The two characters are unrelated etymologically but homophonous in Wang's pun (*li* 禮 / *li* 理).

**8.** I asked: "*The Way-mind is always the master of the body, and the human mind always heeds it.* Following the Master's teaching of 'discrimination and unity,' this saying seems flawed."

The Master said: "Just so. The mind is one. Untainted by the human, it is called the Way-mind; mixed with human falsity, the human mind. The human mind, when it gets its uprightness, *is* the Way-mind. The Way-mind, when it loses its uprightness, *is* the human mind. There were never two minds. Master Cheng said: *The human mind is human desire; the Way-mind is Heaven's principle.* The wording sounds as though it divides them, but the meaning is exact. To say today that 'the Way-mind is master and the human mind heeds' makes them two minds. Heaven's principle and human desire cannot stand together: how could Heaven's principle be the master while human desire goes along, heeding?"

**9.** I asked about Wang Tong (Wenzhongzi) and Han Yu.

The Master said: "Han Yu is just a literary giant. Wenzhongzi is a worthy Confucian. Later generations praise Han Yu for his prose; in truth, he is far below Wenzhongzi."

I asked: "Wenzhongzi has been faulted for his 'imitation of the classics.'"

The Master said: "His imitation of the classics is not entirely to be condemned. Tell me first — how does the writing of later Confucians compare to imitating the classics?"

I said: "Worldly Confucian writing is not free of the wish for fame, but it does aim at making the Way clear; imitation of the classics is more purely fame-seeking."

The Master said: "And to write in order to make the Way clear — what model is one following?"

I said: "Confucius edited the Six Classics in order to make the Way clear."

The Master said: "Then is imitation of the classics not also a way of following Confucius's example?"

I said: "Writing brings new light to the Way; imitating the classics seems merely to mimic their form, with no real benefit to the Way."

The Master said: "Do you take 'making the Way clear' to mean returning the world to plain simplicity, made visible in actual practice — or to mean dressing up one's sentences and squabbling for attention? Great disorder under Heaven follows from empty culture overgrowing real practice. Were the Way clear under Heaven, the *Six Classics* would not need to be transmitted. That Confucius edited them was a thing he was forced to do.

"From Fuxi's drawing of the trigrams down to King Wen and the Duke of Zhou, the writings on the *Changes* — the *Lianshan*, the *Guicang*, and so many others — were so numerous that no one could trace them all, and the doctrine of the *Changes* fell into disorder. Confucius saw that the world's love of literary refinement was growing daily, that the proliferation of texts had no limit, and so he selected the readings of King Wen and the Duke of Zhou and gave them his commentary, holding that *only this catches the lineage*. Then the proliferation died away, and the world's interpreters of the *Changes* spoke with one voice. The same with the *Documents*, the *Odes*, the *Rites*, the *Music*, the *Spring and Autumn Annals*. The *Documents*, after the *Canons* and *Counsels*; the *Odes*, after the *Two Souths*; texts like the *Nine Mounds*, the *Eight Cords*; every kind of licentious or extravagant verse — there must have been thousands. Names and degrees in *Rites* and *Music* multiplied beyond reckoning. Confucius pruned and edited until the rest fell silent. In the *Documents*, the *Odes*, the *Rites*, the *Music*, did Confucius add a single sentence? The chapters of the *Record of Rites* now extant are mostly later Confucian patchwork — not Confucius's text. Even the *Spring and Autumn Annals*, though attributed to Confucius's authorship, was the old text of the Lu state's history. What is called *the brush* meant *brushed in from the old*; what is called *the cutting* meant *cutting out the excess*. There was reduction, not addition. In editing the *Six Classics*, Confucius feared lest excess writing throw the world into disorder. He sought only to simplify, and could only get so far; he wished men to discard the literary in order to seek the real, not to teach by means of literature.

"After the *Spring and Autumn Annals*, literary excess only grew, and the world only grew more disordered. The First Emperor's burning of books was wrong because it was done out of selfish purpose, and because he did not stop at the *Six Classics*. If the aim had been to make the Way clear, then to take all the writings against the canon and against principle and burn them — that, in fact, would have agreed quietly with Confucius's editorial intent. From Qin and Han onward, literature has only flourished. To erase it all is impossible; one ought rather to follow Confucius's method, select what is closest to the truth and bring it to honor, and the wild and perverse will gradually fall away. I do not know exactly what Wang Tong was thinking when he imitated the classics, but I deeply approve of what he did. If a sage were to arise again, he could not change it. The world is not well governed because empty culture flourishes and real practice withers. Each man invents his own opinion, vies for novelty, dazzles common people for praise; thus the eyes and ears of the world are clouded, men give themselves to dressing up their sentences in hopes of being noticed by their age, and forget that there is such a thing as cultivating the root, valuing what is real, returning to plainness. The fault lies with the writers."

I said: "Yet some writings are indispensable. Without the *Zuo Commentary*, the *Spring and Autumn Annals* would be hard to make out."

The Master said: "If the *Spring and Autumn Annals* required a commentary to be intelligible, it would be a lame riddle. Why would a sage make so harsh and obscure a text? The *Zuo Commentary* preserves the old Lu state history. If the *Spring and Autumn* required this to be understood, why did Confucius cut it down?"

I said: "Master Yichuan said: *The commentary is the case-record; the classic is the verdict.* When it says 'so-and-so murdered such-and-such a lord' or 'attacked such-and-such a state,' without knowing the events one cannot deliver the verdict."

The Master said: "Master Yichuan is repeating worldly Confucian opinion; he has not gotten the sage's intent in writing the classic. *Murdered the lord* — the murder of a lord is itself the crime. Why ask further for the details? Punitive expeditions ought to issue from the Son of Heaven; *attacked the state* — the attacking of a state is itself the crime. Why ask further for details? When the sage edited the *Six Classics*, his single aim was to set human hearts upright — to preserve Heaven's principle and remove human desire. On the work of preserving Heaven's principle and removing human desire he did speak; or, when men asked, he spoke as their measure required, and even then he did not say much, fearing they would dwell on the words. So he said, *I would say nothing*. As for matters that indulge desire and obliterate principle — would he set them out in detail to be examined? That would be to lengthen disorder and conduct in evil. Mencius said: *In Confucius's school there was no talk of the affairs of Duke Huan or Duke Wen, and so they were not transmitted to later ages.* That is the family-method of the Confucian house. Worldly Confucians study only the doctrines of the hegemons, and so feel they need to know all those plots and stratagems — pure profit-seeking, the opposite of the sage's intent in editing the classic. How can it ever be reconciled?" He sighed and added: "One cannot speak so to anyone but a man who has reached Heaven's virtue."

The Master said again: "Confucius said, *I have still seen the lacunae in the historians' texts.* Mencius said, *To trust the* Documents *entirely is worse than having no* Documents*; from the* Wu Cheng *I take only two or three slips.* Of the four or five hundred years of Tang, Yu, and Xia, Confucius's editing kept only a few chapters — could there really have been no other events? That he transmits only this, the sage's mind is plain. The sage wished to cut out excess writing; later Confucians want to add more on."

I said: "When the sage made the classics, his aim was to remove human desire and preserve Heaven's principle. As for the affairs of the Five Hegemons and after, the sage did not wish to set them out in detail — that I can see. But even before Yao and Shun, hardly anything appears. Why?"

The Master said: "The age of Fuxi and the Yellow Emperor — its events are sparse, and few transmitted them. From this we can imagine: it must have been an age of complete plain simplicity, with hardly any literary refinement at all. That is the rule of high antiquity, beyond the reach of later generations."

I said: "Texts like the *Three Tombs* did have transmitters. Why did Confucius cut them?"

The Master said: "Even where there were transmitters, with the changing of the age such things were no longer fitting. As atmosphere opened up and refinement flourished, by the late Zhou one could not have rolled the world back to the customs of Xia and Shang — let alone Tang and Yu, let alone Fuxi and the Yellow Emperor. Yet though the rule differed, the Way was one. Confucius reverenced Yao and Shun as ancestors; he held King Wen and King Wu as his standard. The methods of Wen and Wu *are* the Way of Yao and Shun. But because each age must make its own application, the institutions and edicts already differ. Even the works of Xia and Shang, applied in Zhou, no longer fit; that is why the Duke of Zhou *would have combined the three kings, and where there was disagreement, looked up and pondered, day continuing into night*. How then could the rule of high antiquity be reapplied? That is what the sage rightly leaves out."

The Master said again: "If one only commits to non-action and cannot, like the Three Kings, govern by the moment, but insists on enacting the customs of high antiquity, that is the doctrine of the Buddhists and Daoists. Conversely, if one governs by the moment but cannot, like the Three Kings, root every act in the Way, but acts from the heart of profit and gain, then one is a hegemon at most. Many a later Confucian, talking on and on, has talked himself into nothing more than the hegemons' art."

The Master said again: "The rule of Tang and Yu and earlier cannot be restored — leave it alone. The rule of post-Three-Dynasties cannot be patterned — cut it. Only the rule of the Three Dynasties is enactable. Yet those who discuss the Three Dynasties today fail to grasp the root and busy themselves with the branches — and so even *this* cannot be restored."

**10.** I said: "The earlier Confucians, in discussing the *Six Classics*, took the *Spring and Autumn Annals* as 'history.' History records events, which seems somewhat different from the substance of the *Five Classics*."

The Master said: "Spoken of as event, it is *history*; spoken of as Way, it is *classic*. Event is Way and Way is event. The *Spring and Autumn Annals* is also classic; the *Five Classics* are also history. The *Changes* is the history of Fuxi; the *Documents* is the history of Yao, Shun, and after; the *Rites* and *Music* are the history of the Three Dynasties. The events are the same, the Way is the same — where is the difference?"

The Master said again: "The *Five Classics* are also just history. History makes good and evil clear and shows admonition. Of the good that may serve as model, the trace is preserved to teach the rule; of the evil that may serve as warning, the warning is preserved and the affair cut, in order to bar wickedness."

I said: "*Preserving the trace to teach the rule* is also *preserving Heaven's principle as it stands*; *cutting the affair to bar wickedness* is also *checking human desire at its first sprout* — yes?"

The Master said: "When the sages made the classics, this was indeed always the spirit. But do not insist on every wording."

I asked: "If the principle is — *preserve the warning, cut the affair, to bar wickedness* — why did Confucius alone, in the *Odes*, *not* cut the songs of Zheng and Wei? Earlier Confucians say: *evil examples may correct the dissolute heart.* True?"

The Master said: "The *Odes* you have is not Confucius's old text. Confucius said: *Banish the music of Zheng; the music of Zheng is licentious.* And again: *I hate the music of Zheng for its corrupting of the elegant music. The tones of Zheng and Wei are the tones of a perishing state.* That is the family-method of Confucius's house. The three hundred odes Confucius established were all 'elegant music': they could be performed at the suburban and ancestral sacrifices, performed in the village ceremonies; they spread peace and harmony, soaked the nature in virtue, transformed manner and custom — how could they have included these? They lengthen wantonness and lead in wickedness. The licentious must have come in after the Qin burning, when later Confucians stitched in pieces to fill out the count of three hundred. Licentious lyrics are widely loved and easily passed around — see the alleys today. *Evil examples may correct the dissolute heart* is a doctrine cooked up to defend what one cannot defend."

**11.** Sunk for so long in the old way of speaking, I was at first stunned and unsteady when I heard the Master's teaching, with no thread to lay hold of. After a long time of hearing him I began to know the practice of *turning back upon oneself in real action*; only then did I come to trust that the Master's learning was the true descent of the Confucian school, and that everything else was side trail and dead end. Such teachings as *gewu* is the work for sincerity of intent, *making goodness clear* is the work for sincerity of self, *fathoming principle* is the work for fully realizing nature, *treading the path of inquiry* is the work for honoring nature, *broad in culture* is the work for *restrained by ritual*, *utmost discrimination* is the work for *utmost unity* — at first these all sounded scattered and incompatible. Long thinking later, I found my hands and feet were dancing without my noticing.

— *The above recorded by Yueren [Xu Ai].*


**12.** Lu Cheng asked: "On *concentration on the One* — when reading, my whole mind is on the reading; when receiving guests, my whole mind is on the guest. Does that count?"

The Master said: "When you covet a beautiful body, your whole mind is on it; when you covet wealth, your whole mind is on it. Does *that* count? *That* is *chasing the object*, not *concentration on the One*. Concentration on the One is concentration on Heaven's principle alone."

**13.** Asked about *establishing one's resolve*. The Master said: "Only constantly intending to preserve Heaven's principle — *that* is establishing one's resolve. If one can never forget it, in time the mind congeals of itself — what the Daoists call 'forming the sacred embryo.' Out of this single thought of Heaven's principle preserved, eventually beauty, greatness, sageness, divinity unfold. The whole journey is just nourishing and extending this one thought."

**14.** "In the day's work: when one feels scattered, sit quietly. When one feels sluggish about reading, then read. This too is medicine for the disease."

**15.** "With friends: keep yourself a step below them and you gain; keep above and you lose."

**16.** Meng Yuan had a habit of self-righteousness and love of fame. The Master had reproached him for it many times. One day he had just ended a session of admonition when a friend, by way of asking the Master to set him right, was telling about the day's progress in his own work. Yuan, listening, broke in: "That's just what I had figured out long ago." The Master said: "Your sickness is showing again." Yuan's color changed; he was about to argue. The Master said again: "Your sickness is showing." And he explained: "This is the great root-disease of your life. Imagine a piece of land ten paces square, where you have planted one great tree. The rain and dew, the strength of the soil, all go to nourishing this one great root. Off to the side you would like to plant good grain — but the leaves above shade them out, and the roots below tangle them up. How could anything grow? You must cut the tree out, and not leave a thread of root, before you can plant the good seeds. Otherwise, however much you hoe and water, you only feed *that* root."

**17.** Asked: "Does the great quantity of later writing perhaps risk corrupting the orthodox learning?"

The Master said: "The mind of man is, with Heaven's principle, undivided. The sages and worthies, in writing it down, are like portraitists capturing the spirit of a likeness — they can only show shape and broad outline, by which the viewer must hunt for the true thing. The very spirit and air, the laughter and movement, cannot be transmitted. Later writings copy what the sages already roughly drew, then arbitrarily analyze and add to it, showing off their cleverness — and grow further from the truth."

**18.** Asked: "The sage responds to changes inexhaustibly. Surely he must have looked into them in advance?"

The Master said: "How could one look into so much in advance? The sage's mind is like a bright mirror — one bare brightness — and so it responds when struck, and there is no thing it cannot reflect. There is no past form still lingering in it, and no form-yet-to-be already prepared. If the later doctrine were right, it would be wholly opposed to the sage's learning. The Duke of Zhou created the rites and music to show the world — a thing the sage could do; why did Yao and Shun not do it all and leave nothing for him? Confucius edited the *Six Classics* to instruct ten thousand generations — also a thing the sage could do; why did the Duke of Zhou not do it all in advance and leave nothing for Confucius? We must understand: the sage, meeting his time, performs the work that *that time* asks for. Worry only that the mirror be not bright; do not worry whether things will come in to be reflected. Inquiring into changes of affairs is just reflecting the affair when it comes. The student needs to do the *brightening* work first. The student should fear only that this mind cannot become bright — not that the changes of the world cannot all be exhausted."

I said: "But what of the saying *vast and traceless, and yet the myriad images already stand in vivid array*?"

The Master said: "The saying itself is fine. It is only the bad reading that makes it sick."

**19.** "Right principle has no fixed seat and is inexhaustible. What I am saying to you now — do not think that, with a small gain, the matter is closed. Speak it again in ten years, twenty, fifty — there is no end. Sages such as Yao and Shun: above Yao and Shun there is more good without end. Tyrants such as Jie and Zhou: below Jie and Zhou there is more evil without end. Had Jie and Zhou not died, would the evil really have stopped where it stopped? If good had a stopping place, why would King Wen have *gazed at the Way and as if not yet seen it*?"

**20.** Asked: "When still, my thoughts feel right; the moment something happens, I am thrown. Why?"

The Master said: "You only know to nourish in stillness, not the work of overcoming yourself. So when something happens, you topple. One must be polished on the affairs themselves to be able to stand firm — only then is one settled in stillness *and* settled in motion."

**21.** Asked about the work of *going up*.

The Master said: "Later Confucians, the moment the talk turns to the subtle, declare that *going up* is not yet to be studied — go *down* first. So they make 'down' and 'up' two. What the eye can see, the ear hear, the mouth speak, the mind think — that is 'down'; what the eye cannot see, the ear not hear, the mouth not speak, the mind not think — that is 'up.' As with a tree: planting and watering — that is 'down'; the unfolding of growth in the night, the spreading of branches in their season — that is 'up,' and what man could lend his hand to it? So everything one *can* work at and speak of is 'down.' 'Up' is just within 'down.' Everything the sage spoke, however subtle, is 'down.' The student's work in 'down' carries him up of itself; one need not search out a separate work of 'up.'"

**22.** "Holding to one's resolve: like a pain in the heart. With one's whole mind on the pain, where is the leisure for idle talk and idle business?"

**23.** Asked: "How does one work at *utmost discrimination, utmost unity*?"

The Master said: "*Unity* is the aim of *discrimination*; *discrimination* is the work for *unity*. There is no unity beyond discrimination. The character *jing* (discrimination) has 'rice' in it. By way of comparison: to make this rice purely white is the aim of unity. But without the work of pounding, winnowing, sifting, picking out — *discrimination* — it cannot be made purely white. Pounding, winnowing, sifting, picking out is the work of discrimination — and yet it is only for the sake of making the rice purely white, *unity*. *Studying broadly, inquiring carefully, thinking with care, discerning clearly, practicing earnestly* — all are means of *discrimination* in pursuit of *unity*. So too: 'broad in literary culture' is the work of being 'restrained by ritual'; 'investigating things and extending knowing' is the work of 'making intent sincere'; 'treading the path of inquiry' is the work of 'honoring nature'; 'making goodness clear' is the work of 'making oneself sincere' — they are not two doctrines."

**24.** "Knowing is the beginning of acting; acting is the completion of knowing. The sagely learning is one work; knowing and acting cannot be split into two affairs."

**25.** "Qidiao Kai said: *I cannot yet trust myself in this.* The Master was pleased. Zilu set Zigao to be steward of Bi; the Master said, *He robs another's son.* When Zeng Dian said his ambition, the Master assented. The sage's intent is plain in this."

**26.** Asked: "When the mind is at rest in tranquility, may that be called the *centrality before stirring*?"

The Master said: "What people today preserve is only *qi* in a settled state. When they are at rest, only the *qi* is at rest; that cannot be called the centrality before stirring."

I said: "If that is not centrality, is it nevertheless work *toward* centrality?"

The Master said: "It is just removing human desire and preserving Heaven's principle that is the work. In stillness, thought after thought, remove desire and preserve principle; in motion, thought after thought, remove desire and preserve principle — never mind tranquility or restlessness. To rely on tranquility leads gradually to a love of stillness and aversion to motion, with all the many sicknesses lurking inside, never quite extinguished, always sprouting again at the next event. Take *following principle* as one's master, and one is never *not* tranquil. Take *tranquility* as one's master, and one will not necessarily be following principle."

**27.** Asked: "When Confucius's disciples spoke their ambitions, You [Zilu] and Qiu [Ran You] for governance, Gongxi Chi for ritual and music — these are practical affairs of substance. When Zeng Dian spoke, it sounded like play, yet the Master assented. What is going on?"

The Master said: "The three had fixed designs; with fixed designs they leaned to one side — capable in one matter and not necessarily in another. Zeng Dian had no fixed designs. He was just *acting from his proper station, not desiring what lay outside it; in foreign lands acting as in foreign lands, in hardship acting as in hardship — never not at home in himself.* The three are 'vessels' (in Confucius's idiom); Zeng Dian was 'no vessel.' Yet the three were each accomplished, not like the empty babblers of our day; so the Master assented to them as well."

**28.** Asked: "Why does my knowledge make no progress?"

The Master said: "Study must have a root, and one must work at the root. Then one fills out one rank and advances. The Daoists' image of the embryo is good. While in the womb the child is just pure *qi* — what knowledge has it? After birth it cries; then it learns to laugh; then it recognizes father and mother and brothers; then it stands and walks, lifts and carries, until at last there is nothing under Heaven beyond its capacity. The vital *qi* fills out by the day, the sinews strengthen, the hearing and sight open — none of this comes pre-fabricated at birth. So one must have a root. The sage's reaching to *taking up his place in Heaven and Earth, nourishing the myriad creatures* is also nourished from the *centrality before stirring* of joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure. Later Confucians, ignorant of *gewu*, see that the sage knows everything and can do anything, and so they want to ask everything out at the start. How could that be possible?"

He said again: "Establishing one's resolve and working: it is like planting a tree. The root sprouts before the trunk. The trunk before the branches. Branches before leaves. Leaves before flower and fruit. When you first plant the root, simply attend to watering and tending. Do not picture the branches; do not picture the leaves; do not picture the flower; do not picture the fruit. Empty pictures help nothing. Only do not forget the work of planting and watering — and how should there fail to be branches and leaves and flowers and fruit?"

**29.** Asked: "Why can I not understand what I read?"

The Master said: "You hunt only on the surface of the words, and so cannot understand. The old sort of learning could read more and explain more, but its lifelong gain is nothing. One must work in the substance of the mind. When one cannot understand a passage, when an action will not go through, *turn back to one's own mind* and feel out the answer there. The *Four Books* and *Five Classics* are speaking only of this mind-substance. This mind-substance is what is called the Way. When the mind-substance is bright, the Way is bright. There is no second Way. Here is the head of all study."

**30.** "The empty and luminous, undulled — all principles complete, all affairs going forth from it. *No principle outside the mind, no affair outside the mind.*"

**31.** A friend asked: "Master Hui'an [Zhu Xi] said, *What man learns is mind and principle.* What of this saying?"

The Master said: "The mind *is* nature, and nature *is* principle. To set down the word 'and' between them risks making them two. Let the student look at this carefully."

**32.** Someone said: "Everyone has this mind. The mind is principle. Why then do some do good and some do not?"

The Master said: "The minds of evil men have lost their original substance."

**33.** Asked: "*Analyzing in order to reach the utmost subtlety without confusion, then synthesizing in order to fully comprehend the whole without remainder* — what of this?"

The Master said: "Probably not quite right. How would *this* principle admit of analyzing? And what need is there of synthesizing? When the sages spoke of 'discrimination and unity,' that already exhausts it."

**34.** "*Self-examination* is *nourishing oneself in the time of affairs*; *nourishing oneself* is *self-examination in the time of no affairs*."

**35.** Cheng [Lu Cheng] once asked about Xiangshan's [Lu Jiuyuan's] teaching of *working on the changing course of human feelings and affairs*.

The Master said: "Apart from human feelings and affairs there are no affairs. Are joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure not human feelings? From seeing, hearing, speaking, moving, on through wealth and want, hardship and life and death — all are 'affairs.' And the affairs are within human feelings. The whole point comes down to *bringing centrality and harmony to fruition*; and that comes down to *being watchful when alone*."

**36.** Cheng asked: "Are the names *humaneness, righteousness, ritual, wisdom* given because of what has *issued forth*?"

The Master said: "Yes."

Another day Cheng said: "*Pity, shame, deference, and the sense of right* — are these the *outward virtues* of the nature?"

The Master said: "*Humaneness, righteousness, ritual, wisdom* are also the outward virtues. Nature is one. Spoken of in its bodily form, we call it *Heaven*; spoken of as governor, we call it *Lord*; spoken of as flowing forth, we call it *mandate*; conferred on man, we call it *nature*; presiding over the body, we call it *mind*. As mind sends itself forth: meeting one's father, we call it filiality; meeting one's lord, we call it loyalty. From there the names are inexhaustible — but it is one nature only. Just as a man is one person: opposite his father he is called *son*; opposite his son he is called *father*. The names go on without end, but he is one man only. The point for the student is to work in *nature*; once one sees clearly the single character *nature*, the myriad principles light up at once."

**37.** Once, in discussing the work of study, the Master said: "In teaching, one cannot hold to a single bias. At first the mind is wild as a monkey, restless as a horse, uncatchable, and most of its thoughts are on the side of human desire. So at first one teaches such a person to sit quietly and quiet his thinking. After a long while, when the mind has settled somewhat, to merely sit there, hollow and watchful, like dry wood and dead ashes, is also of no use; one must then teach the work of self-examination and overcoming. Such work has no time off; like clearing out thieves, one must intend the broom-clean sweep. When at rest, take love of beautiful women, of money, of fame, one by one, dig out their roots, and pull them up so that they may never sprout again — only then may one rest. Be like a cat watching the mouse: one eye always open, one ear always pricked, the moment a thought arises, overcome it. With axe-edge and hammer, do not give it room or grace; do not hide it; do not give it a path of escape. *That* is real work; *that* will sweep clean. When at last there is no selfishness left to overcome, one stands at ease. The saying *what is there to think about, what is there to plan?* is not for beginners. The beginner must think, must examine, must overcome — that *is* sincerity in thought, thinking only of one Heaven's principle. Once Heaven's principle is whole, *what is there to think about, what is there to plan?* arrives of itself."

**38.** Cheng asked: "What of one who is afraid of ghosts at night?"

The Master said: "Ordinarily he has not collected up his rightness, and his mind has lapses; that is why he fears. If one's daily conduct accords with the spirits, what is there to fear?"

Zixin said: "An upright ghost need not be feared; but an evil ghost would not care whether a man was good or bad — so one cannot help but fear."

The Master said: "How could an evil ghost confound an upright man? The fear itself is the evil, not the ghost — and so the man is confounded. The ghost did not confound him; he confounded himself. So with all of us: if we love a beautiful body, we are confounded by the lust-ghost; if we love wealth, by the greed-ghost; angry where one ought not to be angry, by the rage-ghost; afraid where one ought not to be afraid, by the fear-ghost."

**39.** "*Settledness* is the original substance of the mind, which is Heaven's principle; motion and stillness are the times that meet it."

**40.** Cheng asked about the differences between the *Great Learning* and the *Doctrine of the Mean*. The Master said: "Zisi summed up the meaning of the *Great Learning* in the opening chapter of the *Doctrine of the Mean*."

**41.** Asked about Confucius's *rectification of names*: "The earlier Confucians say it would have meant '*memorialize to the Son of Heaven, then to the regional lord; depose Zhe and install Ying.*' What of this?"

The Master said: "Surely not. Could there be — when a man has, with the deepest reverence and full ritual, treated me as his counselor in government — that I should immediately move to depose him? Could that be human feeling, or Heaven's principle? Confucius, having consented to take part in Zhe's government, must already have had Zhe's whole-hearted entrusting of the state to him. The sage's perfect virtue and utmost sincerity must already have transformed Wei Zhe so that he saw he could not be a man without acknowledging his father, and would have wept and run to fetch his father back. The love of father and son is rooted in Heaven's nature. With Zhe so genuinely repentant, how could Kuaikui not be moved? When Kuaikui returned, Zhe would offer up the state and beg punishment; Kuaikui, transformed by his son and conciliated by Confucius's perfect sincerity, would surely refuse and command Zhe back to the throne. The ministers and people, too, would want Zhe as their lord; and Zhe would publicly confess his crime, petition the Son of Heaven, inform the regional lords, and insist on offering the state to his father. Kuaikui and the ministers and people, in turn, would praise Zhe's repentant filial beauty, petition the Son of Heaven, inform the regional lords, and insist that Zhe must be lord. Then the mandate would be gathered in Zhe again and he would resume rule of Wei. Zhe, unable to refuse, would honor Kuaikui as Grand Father — like the abdicated emperors of later ages — and provide ritual care for him, and then take back the throne. Thus *lord lord, minister minister, father father, son son*; *the names rectified, the words right* — and at one stroke government becomes possible under Heaven. The 'rectification of names' might just have been this."

**42.** Cheng was lodging in the Court of State Ceremonial when news came that his son was gravely ill. He grew so heavy with worry that he could not bear it.

The Master said: "Now is the very time to work. If you let *this* moment pass, what is the use of discussing learning when nothing is happening? A man should be polished at *this* sort of moment. A father's love for his son is the deepest of feelings. Yet Heaven's principle has its own measure of centrality and harmony; to exceed is selfish thought. Most men, faced with such a moment, take it for Heaven's principle that one *should* be in agony and so they let the agony run on, not realizing that *being burdened by anxiety* is already to lose one's uprightness. Of the seven feelings, the trouble is mostly with excess, rarely with deficiency. Once excessive, it is no longer the original substance of the mind. One must moderate, find the mean. As at a parent's funeral: would not a son wish to weep himself to death and find his heart eased? And yet the rule is *grief shall not destroy life* — not because the sage forced restraint, but because the substance of Heaven's principle has its own limits, and one must not exceed. One needs only to recognize the substance of the mind; nothing of it can be added or taken from."

**43.** "It is wrong to say that the *centrality before stirring* is something everyone has. Substance and function are one source: where there is *that* substance, there is *that* function. Where there is the centrality before stirring, there is the *harmony of all hitting the proper measure when stirred*. People today do not have the harmony of stirring rightly because their centrality before stirring is also not yet whole."

**44.** "The *Changes*'s words are 'the first nine, the dragon submerged, do not act' — six characters. The image of the *Changes* is the first stroke of the diagram. The change of the *Changes* is when its season is full. The divination of the *Changes* is in the use of its words."

**45.** "*Night qi* is spoken with reference to the ordinary man. When the student really works, then in the day, in affairs and out of affairs, *this* qi is gathered and unfolds. With the sage, no need to speak of night qi at all."

**46.** Cheng asked about the chapter *Take it and it stays; let it go and it is gone*: "*It comes and it goes without time; no one knows where it dwells.* This is said of the ordinary mind. The student must also know that the original substance of the mind was originally so. Then the work of *taking* has no sickness in it. Do not say 'going out' is being lost and 'coming in' is being preserved. As to its original substance, originally it has no going out or coming in. As to its going out and coming in, what runs forth in thought and use is 'going out,' yet the *master* is always luminously here — what 'going out' is there? And without going out, what 'coming in'? Master Cheng's 'cavity' is just Heaven's principle. Even amid all-day comings and goings, if one does not depart from Heaven's principle, one is *in the cavity*. To depart from Heaven's principle — *that* is to be 'released,' that is to be 'gone.'"

He said again: "*Going out and coming in* is just motion and stillness. Motion and stillness have no end; what 'where' is there?"

**47.** Wang Jiaxiu asked: "The Buddhists draw men into the Way by promising release from birth and death; the Daoists draw men by promising long life. Their hearts do not aim at making men do ill; pursued to its limit, each catches sight of the *upper half* of the sage. But it is not the right road into the Way — like men entering office through irregular channels, however high they climb, the way is wrong, and the gentleman does not take it. The Daoists and Buddhists, taken to their height, almost coincide with the Confucian, but they have taken hold of the upper half and dropped the lower; they fall short of the sage's wholeness. Yet that the upper half is shared — this cannot be denied. Later Confucians, in turn, hold only to the lower half, fragmenting it and losing its truth, ending in mere recitation, ornament, profit-seeking, and exegetical detail — also heretical. These four schools labor a lifetime in vain. Compared with the Daoists and Buddhists — pure of heart, light of want, lifted above worldly burden — the Confucians of today look the worse. Now the student need not first denounce Daoism and Buddhism: let him devote himself to the sage's learning. When the sage's learning is clear, Daoism and Buddhism will fade of themselves. Otherwise, what we are studying may be a thing those others would not stoop to, and we will be asking *them* to stoop. Hard going. So I see it. What does the Master say?"

The Master said: "What you say is roughly right. But to speak of an 'upper half' and a 'lower half' is itself a one-sided view. In the sage's Way of the Great Mean and Utmost Rectitude, top and bottom are one thread; what 'upper' and 'lower' is there? *One yin, one yang — that is the Way.* But the humane look at it and call it humaneness; the wise look at it and call it wisdom; the common people use it daily and do not know — and so the gentleman's Way is rare. Are humaneness and wisdom not also Way? Only, looking from a single angle, one falls into bias and into sickness."

**48.** "The yarrow stalks are *Changes*; the tortoise is *Changes* too."

**49.** Asked: "Confucius said King Wu was *not yet wholly good* — he must have had some dissatisfaction?"

The Master said: "For King Wu, that was just so."

Asked: "Had King Wen not yet died, what then?"

The Master said: "King Wen in his time already held two thirds of the world. Had he lived to King Wu's time of campaign against Shang, he might not have raised troops at all — that other third would surely have come over of its own. King Wen was simply masterful in handling Zhou the tyrant, not letting him give full play to his evil."

**50.** Asked about Mencius's saying *holding to the mean without weighing is just like holding to one fixed point*.

The Master said: "*The mean* is just Heaven's principle, just *change*. It changes with the time. How can one *hold* to it? One must respond to the time and apply it suitably. A fixed rule cannot be set in advance. The later Confucians' wish to articulate principle without seam, to set down a fixed pattern — that is the true holding to one point."

**51.** Tang Xu asked: "Establishing one's resolve — is it preserving a thought of doing good and removing evil?"

The Master said: "When the good thought is preserved, *that* is Heaven's principle. *That* thought is good; what further good is there to think? *That* thought is not evil; what evil is there to remove? *That* thought is like the root-sprout of a tree. To establish one's resolve is to keep this good thought standing, that is all. *Following the heart's desire without overstepping the rule* is just resolve grown to ripeness."

**52.** "Spirit, virtue, words, action — by and large, gather them in is the master, scatter only when one must. Heaven, earth, men, beings — all of them likewise."

**53.** Asked: "What sort of man was Wenzhongzi?"

The Master said: "Wenzhongzi nearly had the whole substance, only on a smaller scale — alas that he died young!"

Asked: "What of his fault, the *Continuation of the Classics*?"

The Master said: "His Continuation cannot be entirely condemned."

Asked further. After a long silence: "I feel the more, what bitter labor that fine craftsman undertook in his solitude."

**54.** "Xu Heng's saying that *the Confucian must put earning a living first* — that doctrine misleads people."

**55.** Asked about the Daoists' *primal qi*, *primal spirit*, *primal essence*.

The Master said: "All one thing. Flowing forth, it is *qi*; gathered, it is *essence*; subtle in use, it is *spirit*."

**56.** "Joy, anger, sorrow, pleasure — the original substance is centered and harmonious. The moment we ourselves add a thought, we go too far or too little — and *that* is selfishness."

**57.** Asked: "*When weeping, one does not sing.*" The Master said: "The substance of the sage's mind is naturally so."

**58.** "Overcoming oneself must be a clean sweep. So long as a thread remains, all the evils call each other in."

**59.** Asked about *the New Book of Pitch-Pipes*.

The Master said: "The student should attend to what is urgent. To master those calculations is, even so, perhaps not useful, unless the root of ritual and music has first been set in the heart. Take, for instance, the technique of using pipes filled with ash to detect the seasons: yet at the very moment of the winter solstice, the rising of the ash in the pipe may come before or after the precise instant — in such a sliver of time, who knows whether the pipe is right at the solstice's instant? You need first to know the solstice's instant in your own heart. Just there, the technique cannot pass through. The student should put his work first into the root of ritual and music."

**60.** Yueren said: "The mind is like a mirror. The sage's mind is like a bright mirror; the ordinary man's like a clouded one. The current account of *gewu* is like polishing a mirror by working at the reflections — but the mirror is still clouded; how can it reflect? The Master's *gewu* is to polish the mirror itself, working at the polishing. Once the mirror is bright, reflection is never neglected."

**61.** Asked about the fineness and coarseness of the Way.

The Master said: "The Way is neither fine nor coarse; what is fine or coarse is *the seeing*. As when one enters a room: at first one only sees the rough shape; after a while, one sees the columns and walls clearly; longer still, even the carved ornaments on a column appear. Yet it is one room only."

**62.** The Master said: "Of late you have asked few questions. Why? When a man does not work, he assumes he already knows, and that to study is just to follow what he already has. He does not realize that selfish desires arise daily — like dust on the ground; one day unswept and there is another layer. Real work makes one see that the Way is endless: the deeper one searches, the deeper it goes. One must press until not a thread of thinly veiled brightness has been spared."

**63.** Asked: "*Knowledge fully reached, then sincerity of intent can be spoken of.* Now Heaven's principle and human desire I do not yet know exhaustively. How can I work at overcoming?"

The Master said: "If a man works in real, urgent, near-himself fashion without ceasing, then the subtleties of Heaven's principle in this mind will appear day by day, and the subtleties of selfish desire too will appear day by day. Without overcoming, all-day talk gets nowhere — Heaven's principle never appears, selfish desire never appears. Like walking a road — having walked one stretch, one knows that stretch; at a forking, ask the way, then walk again — and so step by step one reaches the place. Today people will not preserve the Heaven's principle they already know, will not remove the human desire they already know, and only fret that they cannot know it all. Idle talk — what good is it? Wait until you have nothing left to overcome, and then you may worry about not yet knowing all. It will not be too late."

**64.** Asked: "*The Way is one, that is all.* The ancients' discussions often differ. Is there a key to seeking it?"

The Master said: "The Way has no fixed seat or shape; one cannot grasp it. Yet to bind oneself in the literal text is to grow far from it. People speak of *Heaven*, but who has actually seen Heaven? To say only that sun, moon, wind, thunder are Heaven — wrong; to say that man, beasts, plants are not Heaven — also wrong. The Way *is* Heaven. Once recognized, where is it not? But each man takes his corner, fixes it as the whole Way, and so the differences. If one knows to seek inward, to see one's own mind-substance — there is no time and no place where this is not the Way. From beginning to end of all time, no end and no beginning — what same and different is there? *The mind is the Way; the Way is Heaven; to know the mind is to know the Way and to know Heaven.*"

He said again: "Gentlemen, if you wish to see this Way truly, you must do it through your own mind — not borrow from outside."

**65.** Asked: "And the names of things, the measures and degrees — must they not also first be looked into?"

The Master said: "Once the substance of one's mind is brought to completion, the use is within it. If the mind-substance is nourished, with a real centrality before stirring, there is naturally a stirred-and-rightly-measured harmony — and naturally there is nothing one cannot apply oneself to. Without this mind, however many names and degrees one has memorized, they remain unrelated to oneself, mere ornament; in the moment one cannot put them through. Yet not that one ignores names and degrees: only — *know what comes first and what after, and one is near the Way*."

He said again: "Each man should fulfill his own ability. *Ability* is what one is fitted to do — Kui's music, Ji's farming were what their endowments suited. To bring them to fulfillment is to make their mind-substance pure Heaven's principle. The use of it then issues out of Heaven's principle — *that* is what we call ability. Once the mind-substance is purely Heaven's principle, one is also *no vessel*: even Kui and Ji, asked to switch crafts, would have been able. Likewise *acting from one's station — from wealth, from poverty, from hardship* — that is being *no vessel*. Only the man whose mind-substance is upright can do it."

**66.** "Better a few feet of well-water with a source than acres of pondless water without one — life never runs out." (The Master was sitting by a pond; there was a well nearby; that is why he used the comparison.)

**67.** Asked: "The world declines daily. How can the airs of high antiquity be seen again?"

The Master said: "One day is one *yuan*. When at dawn one rises, sits, and has not yet taken up affairs — the bright clear scene of this mind is itself like roaming in Fuxi's age."

**68.** Asked: "If the mind keeps chasing things, what is to be done?"

The Master said: "When the prince sits poised in clear repose, and the Six Ministers each have their charge, the realm is well governed. The mind directs the five organs, and ought to be the same. Now, when the eye wants to see, the mind chases off into color; when the ear wants to hear, the mind chases off into sound — like a prince selecting officers, going himself to sit in the Ministry of Personnel; or mobilizing troops, going himself to sit in the Ministry of War. He thereby loses his ruling place — and the Six Ministers also lose theirs."

**69.** "When a good thought arises, know it and fill it out; when an evil thought arises, know it and stop it. *Knowing*, *filling out*, *stopping* — these belong to the *resolve*, the *bright hearing of Heaven*. The sage has only this. The student should preserve this."

**70.** Cheng said: "The minds of love of beauty, love of wealth, love of fame are surely selfish desires. But what about idle scattered thoughts — how are they *desire*?"

The Master said: "They have their roots in the love of beauty, of wealth, of fame; if you trace each to its root, you will see. In your own mind, you definitely know that you have no thought of becoming a thief — why? Because you have no original heart for it. If your mind regarding wealth, beauty, and fame were as devoid of root as your mind regarding theft, then there would be no scattered thoughts — only the original substance of the mind, with what idle thoughts would there be? *That* is *still and unstirred*; *that* is the centrality before stirring; *that* is the *vast and all-seeing impartiality*. Naturally it then *responds and is fully connected*; naturally it stirs and hits the proper measure; naturally things come and are met."

**71.** Asked about *the resolve at the helm, the qi second*.

The Master said: "*Where the resolve goes, the qi follows* — that is what is meant; not 'the resolve is primary, the qi a vice.' Hold to one's resolve, and the qi is nourished within. Do not coarsen the qi, and one is *also* holding to one's resolve. Mencius is rescuing Gaozi from a bias, and so speaks dialectically."

**72.** Asked: "An earlier Confucian said: *The Way of the sage must descend to humble itself; the worthy man's words pull him to a height.* What of this?"

The Master said: "Not so. Were it so, the sage would be putting on a show. The sage is like Heaven: there is no place where he is not Heaven. Above the three luminaries — Heaven; below the nine layers — Heaven. When did Heaven ever 'descend to humble itself'? *That* is what is meant by 'great and so transformed.' The worthy is like a tall mountain — he holds his height. But a hundred-fathom mountain cannot stretch to a thousand fathoms; a thousand cannot to ten thousand: it is not that the worthy *pulls himself up*. To pull himself up would be falsity."

**73.** Asked: "Yichuan held that one should not *seek the centrality before joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure are stirred*. Master Yanping had students *look at the airs before stirring*. How are they?"

The Master said: "Both are right. Yichuan feared men would *grasp* a centrality before stirring as if it were a thing, take it for the *qi* settled in stillness, and so he taught only nourishing and self-examination. Yanping feared that men had no place to lay their hands, and so taught seeking the airs before stirring at every moment, fixing the eye and ear there — which is the very work of *being vigilant where one cannot see, apprehensive where one cannot hear*. These are the ancients' makeshift words for drawing men in."

Cheng asked: "Joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure — the wholeness of centrality and harmony, the ordinary man certainly does not have. But for one small affair where joy and anger are warranted, when one had no prior heart of joy or anger, and yet at the moment one hits the right measure — may that be called centrality and harmony?"

The Master said: "In one moment, in one affair, yes, you can call it centrality and harmony; but not the *Great Root and Comprehending Way*. Human nature is good; centrality and harmony are what every man originally has — how can one say no? But when the ordinary man's mind has its cloud-cover, the substance only sometimes flashes out — momentarily bright, momentarily extinguished — never the wholeness. Only when there is no place where one is not centered may one speak of the *Great Root*; no place where one is not harmonious — the *Comprehending Way*. Only the utmost sincerity of the world can establish the world's Great Root."

Cheng said: "I am not yet clear on the meaning of *centrality*."

The Master: "You must feel it out from the substance of your mind; words cannot convey it. *Centrality* is just Heaven's principle."

— "What is Heaven's principle?"

— "When you have removed human desire, you know Heaven's principle."

— "Why is Heaven's principle called *centrality*?"

— "Because it leans nowhere."

— "What air is it, *to lean nowhere*?"

— "Like a bright mirror — its whole body crystal, with not a hair of dust clinging."

— "*To lean* would be to have something clinging. As clinging to love of beauty, of wealth, of fame — only then can leaning be seen. But before stirring, beauty and wealth and fame are not yet attached — how is it known whether one is leaning?"

— "Although nothing is attached at the moment, the heart of love of beauty, of wealth, of fame, has not been absent in ordinary days. Once *not absent*, then it is *present*; and once present, leaning cannot be denied. Like a malarial patient: though he does not have his fits at every moment, the root of the disease has not been removed, and we cannot call him a sound man. One must, in ordinary days, sweep clean every selfish heart — love of beauty, of wealth, of fame — without a thread remaining; only when this mind, in its whole substance, is opened wide and is purely Heaven's principle, may one speak of the *centrality before joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure are stirred*. *That* is the world's Great Root."

**74.** Asked: "*With Yan Hui's death the sage's learning was lost.* This saying I cannot but doubt."

The Master said: "Only Yan Hui saw the wholeness of the sage's Way. Watch his sigh: *The Master orders me on step by step, broadens me with culture, restrains me by ritual.* That is said *after* the seeing-through. *Broadening with culture, restraining by ritual* — how is *that* an 'ordering on step by step'? The student must ponder. The full body of the Way is hard for even the sage to convey to others; the student must cultivate himself and awaken himself. Yan Hui *wished to follow but could find no path* — that *is* King Wen's *gazing at the Way and as if not yet seeing it*. *Gazing as if not yet seen* is true seeing. With Yan Hui's death, the orthodox lineage was no longer fully transmitted."

**75.** Asked: "*The body's master is the mind; the mind's clear lighting is knowing; what knowing sends forth is intent; where intent rests is thing.* Is that right?"

The Master said: "Roughly so."

**76.** "To preserve only this mind, always present — *that* is study. Past and future affairs — what does it help to think of them? It only releases the mind."

**77.** "Words without order also reveal the mind unpreserved."

**78.** Shangqian asked about Mencius's *unmoved mind* as different from Gaozi's.

The Master said: "Gaozi clamps the mind down hard, will not let it move; Mencius wants to *gather rightness* until naturally unmoved."

He said again: "The original substance of the mind is naturally unmoved. The original substance of the mind is *nature*; nature is *principle*. Nature does not, originally, move; principle does not, originally, move. *Gathering rightness* is restoring the original substance of the mind."

**79.** "*The myriad images stand vivid* — at the same moment *vast and traceless*; *vast and traceless* — at the same moment *the myriad images stand vivid*. *Vast and traceless* is the father of unity; *the myriad images vivid* is the mother of discrimination. Within unity is discrimination; within discrimination is unity."

**80.** "*No thing outside the mind.* If my mind sends forth a thought of filiality toward parents, *that filiality* is a thing."

**81.** The Master said: "Today, those who study what I call *gewu* still mostly fall into ear-and-mouth talk. How much more those whose study is mere ear-and-mouth — could they turn this way? Heaven's principle, human desire — at their fineness, one must constantly work at examining and overcoming, and only then will sights gradually appear. As we speak now of Heaven's principle, who knows how many selfish desires have flickered up in the heart in the meantime — some sneaking up unnoticed; even with effort one does not easily see them. How much less can one know all by mouth-talk? People keep talking up Heaven's principle and laying it aside without following; talking up human desire and laying it aside without removing — what *gewu* and *zhizhi* learning is that? Later learning, at its peak, manages only the work of *taking rightness by surprise*."

**82.** Asked about *gewu*.

The Master said: "*Ge* is *to make right*. Make right the not-right, returning it to right."

**83.** Asked: "*To know where to rest* is to know that the supreme good is in my mind, not outside, and so resolve is settled?"

The Master said: "Yes."

**84.** Asked: "Does one work at *gewu* in motion only?"

The Master said: "*Gewu* knows no division between motion and stillness. Stillness is also a thing. Mencius's *there must always be activity* applies to motion and stillness both."

**85.** "The hardest of the work lies in *gewu* and *zhizhi*. That is the very business of making intent sincere. With intent sincere, the mind is generally already upright, the body already cultivated. But the work of rectifying the mind and cultivating the body each has its place: cultivating the body is on the *issued forth* side; rectifying the mind is on the *not yet issued* side. Mind upright is centered; body cultivated is harmonious."

**86.** "From *gewu* and *zhizhi* up through *pacifying the world*, all is one *illuminating bright virtue*. Even *loving the people* is a matter of bright virtue. *Bright virtue* is the virtue of the mind, which is humaneness. The humane man takes Heaven and earth and the myriad things as one body. If a single thing fails to find its place, my humaneness has not been completed."

**87.** "To say only *illuminate bright virtue* without *love the people* slants toward Daoism and Buddhism."

**88.** "*The supreme good* is nature. Nature has not a thread of evil; therefore *supreme good*. To rest in it is just to return to its original suchness."

**89.** Asked: "*Knowing the supreme good is my nature; my nature is in my mind; my mind is the place where the supreme good rests* — therefore one is no longer scattered in outward seeking, and resolve is settled. Settled, one is not disturbed and is still. Still, not stirring rashly, one is at peace. At peace, with all of one's mind here, with thousands of thoughts on this single matter, surely able to deliberate, one obtains the supreme good. Is that the picture?"

The Master said: "Roughly."

**90.** Asked: "Master Cheng said *the humane man takes Heaven, earth, and the myriad things as one body*. Why then is Mozi's *love without distinction* not called humaneness?"

The Master said: "This is hard to put. One must work it out for oneself. Humaneness is the ceaseless principle of generation. It is everywhere; yet its flowing has its own gradient — therefore *generation without ceasing*. As at the winter solstice, when the first yang is born, it must come from one yang's birth, and only then proceeds to the six yangs; without that one yang, how could there be six? The same with yin. *Because* there is gradation, there is a starting-point; *because* there is a starting-point, there is generation; *because* there is generation, there is no ceasing. Like a tree: when it first puts forth a sprout — *that* is its sap-of-life starting; the sprout, then the trunk; the trunk, then branches and leaves; only then is there generation without ceasing. Without sprout, what trunk, what branch and leaf? The fact that it sprouts means a root below. With a root, generation; without, death. Without root, what sprouts? Father–son and brother–brother love is the sap-of-life starting in the human heart; from there to humaneness toward the people, and care for things — branches and leaves and trunk. Mozi's *love without distinction* makes one's father and brother and the stranger on the road equal — and so *has no starting-point*. Not sprouting, you know there is no root; not generating without ceasing — how can it be called humaneness? *Filial reverence and fraternal deference are the root of humaneness* — humaneness's principle generates from within."

**91.** Asked: "Master Yanping said *one should follow principle and have no selfish heart*. What is the difference between *following principle* and *no selfish heart*?"

The Master said: "The mind is principle. *No selfish heart* is precisely *following principle*. *Not following principle* is precisely *selfish heart*. To analyze mind and principle as though they were two — perhaps not best."

Asked again: "The Buddhist is unstained by all the world's private feelings and desires — it seems he has no selfish heart. But he abandons the human relationships outside, and so he does *not* follow principle."

The Master: "The two are one matter — both go to making good a single private self of his own."

**92.** Kan asked: "*Holding to resolve like a heartache — with one's whole mind on the pain, what leisure for idle words and idle business?*"

The Master said: "For a beginner, this kind of work is fine; but one must learn that *coming and going are without time, no one knows where it dwells*. The mind's lighted spirit is by nature like this — only such work has somewhere to land. To merely guard tightly may produce a sickness in the work itself."

**93.** Kan asked: "If one only does nourishment and not 'looking into,' may not desire be mistaken for principle? Then what?"

The Master said: "One must know what study is. Looking-into is also nourishment. Not looking-into is just that the will to nourish is not deep enough."

— "What is *knowing what study is*?"

— "Tell me, what is one studying for? What is one studying?"

— "I have heard the Master teach: study is studying to preserve Heaven's principle. The original substance of the mind *is* Heaven's principle. To embody Heaven's principle is just to keep one's mind without selfish thought."

— "Then one need only overcome selfish thought — what worries about principle and desire being unclear?"

— "I just fear I cannot recognize selfish thought truly."

— "It is just that the will is not deep enough. With will deep enough, the eye sees and the ear hears all here — how can one not recognize? *The heart of right and wrong every man has*; one need not seek it from outside. Looking into is just embodying what one's mind sees — there is no separate seeing outside the mind."

**94.** The Master asked friends in the hall: "How has the work been of late?"

One friend said something about empty brightness.

The Master: "That is talking about scenery."

Another spoke of difference between past and present.

The Master: "That is talking about effects."

The two were perplexed.

The Master: "Today's work for our group is just for the heart of doing good to be real and pressing. With it real and pressing, on seeing good one moves; on a fault one corrects — *that* is real and pressing work. So human desire daily declines, Heaven's principle daily brightens. To keep chasing scenery and effects is *helping it grow* and *running outward*. Sickness, not work."

**95.** Friends reading often picked at Hui'an [Zhu Xi]. The Master said: "Where there is intent to differ, that itself is wrong. My differing from Hui'an at the *first step* is by hairs and miles, and one cannot but distinguish. But my mind and Hui'an's mind have never been at odds. Where his exegesis is sound, how could one move a single character?"

**96.** Xiyuan asked: "The sage may be reached by study. But Bo Yi and Yi Yin were finally not equal to Confucius in talent; how can they all be called sages?"

The Master said: "What makes a sage a sage is that his mind is purely Heaven's principle, with no admixture of human desire. As what makes pure gold *pure* is that its assay is full, with no copper or lead. A man pure in Heaven's principle is a sage; gold of full assay is pure. Yet sages do differ in capacity — as gold differs in mass. Yao and Shun were ten thousand *yi*; King Wen and Confucius nine thousand; Yu, Tang, and King Wu seven or eight thousand; Bo Yi and Yi Yin four or five thousand. Capacity differed; pure Heaven's principle was the same — all are called sages. Just as the mass differed, but full assay is the same: all are called pure gold. Place a five-thousand-*yi* sage among the ten-thousand-*yi* sages; the assay is the same. Place Yi and Yin between Yao and Confucius; the purity of Heaven's principle is the same. What makes pure gold is the assay, not the mass; what makes a sage is purity in Heaven's principle, not capacity. So even an ordinary man, if he applies himself to study and makes this mind purely Heaven's principle, may also be a sage — as a single ounce of gold compared to ten thousand *yi*: the masses are wide apart, but the *full-assay* point may be reached without shame. Therefore *all men may be Yao and Shun*. To study sagehood is just to remove human desire and preserve Heaven's principle, as smelting gold seeks the full assay. The closer the assay to full, the easier the smelting; the lower the assay, the harder. Of human nature, clear or muddy, fine or mixed — there are middle-and-up and middle-and-down. In their relation to the Way: *knowing by birth, acting with ease*; *knowing by learning, acting for benefit*; the lowest must do *what others do once, a hundred times; what others ten times, a thousand times*. When successful, all are one. Later generations, not knowing that what makes a sage is purity in Heaven's principle, look only at knowledge and capacity. Believing the sage knows everything and can do everything, one says, 'I must master his many knowledges and capacities one by one.' Not laboring on Heaven's principle, men exhaust spirit and strength on bookish exegesis, on hunting names and degrees, on imitating outward forms; knowledge widens, desire grows; talent multiplies, principle dims. Like one who sees a man of ten thousand *yi* of pure gold and, instead of smelting his own to full assay so as not to be ashamed of his lesser mass, is wildly intent on matching the mass, throws in tin, lead, copper, iron — the mass grows, the assay sinks; in the end no gold is left." — Yueren beside the Master said: "This image breaks the worldly Confucian's confusion at the root, a great service to the student." The Master said again: "Our work is to seek the *daily reduction*, not the *daily increase*. To reduce by one part of human desire is to recover one part of Heaven's principle. How light, how unbound, how simple!"

**97.** Shi De asked: "On *gewu*, the Master's teaching is plain and accessible — anyone can see it. Why did Wenzhong [Zhu Xi], a man of unequalled brilliance, miss it?"

The Master said: "Master Wenzhong had great vigor of spirit. Already in his early years he was bent on *carrying on the past and opening to the future*, so he set himself to inquiry and writing. Had he first worked on himself in the urgent way, he would not have had leisure for that. After he had grown in virtue, he did indeed worry that the Way was not clear. As Confucius in his retirement edited the *Six Classics* — pruning and simplifying for later students — and did not need much textual research. Wenzhong, in his youth, wrote so many books; in old age he came to regret it as having been done backwards." Shi De said: "The late regret — when he said *the awakening to my old fixed text*, and again *however well I read, what good is it to my actual matter?*, and again *this has nothing to do with hugging books and clinging to words* — these show that he saw at last that he had been wrong, and turned to the urgent self-cultivation."

The Master: "Yes — this is where Wenzhong cannot be matched. He had such force that one regret could turn him. Pity that he did not live long after; many earlier mistakes he could not amend."

**98.** Kan was clearing weeds among the flowers and said: "Why is good in Heaven and earth so hard to grow, and evil so hard to remove?"

The Master said: "Because they have not yet been grown, not yet been removed." After a while: "Such a way of looking at good and evil starts from the bodily form, and so goes wrong." Kan did not yet understand.

The Master: "The vital intent of Heaven and earth treats flowers and weeds alike — when did it ever divide them into good and evil? You wish to look at the flowers, so the flowers become 'good' and the weeds 'evil'; if you wished to use the weeds, you would call the weeds 'good.' Such good-and-evil are born of your mind's likes and dislikes, and so we know it is wrong."

— "Then is there *no good and no evil*?"

— "*No good and no evil* is the stillness of principle; *good and evil* is the motion of *qi*. When one is unmoved by *qi*, *that* is no good and no evil — and *that* is *the supreme good*."

— "The Buddhists also speak of no good and no evil. How does this differ?"

— "The Buddhists fix on *no good and no evil* and so let everything go — they cannot govern the world. The sage's *no good and no evil* is just *no fabricated likes and no fabricated dislikes, no movement by* qi. Yet *following the kingly Way, attaining its perfect symmetry*, one *circulates Heaven's principle* and has *the principle of accomplishment and aid*."

— "If weeds are not evil, then weeds should not be removed."

— "Now you slip into Daoism and Buddhism. If the weeds obstruct, what harm is there in your removing them?"

— "Then *making likes, making dislikes* again?"

— "*Not making likes and dislikes* does not mean having no likes and dislikes — that would be a man without consciousness. *Not making* is just that likes and dislikes follow principle, with not a hair of added intent. So it is as if one had not 'liked' or 'disliked' at all."

— "How is removing weeds 'following principle, without intent'?"

— "If the weeds are an obstacle, principle says to remove them — and one removes them. If one happens not to remove them yet, the heart is not burdened. If one adds a hair of intent, the mind-substance suffers a stain, with much *moved-by-qi*."

— "Then good and evil are not in things at all?"

— "They are in your mind. *Following principle* is good; *moved by* qi *is* evil."

— "After all, things have no good and evil?"

— "It is so in the mind, so also in the things. Worldly Confucians not knowing this, abandoning mind to chase things, take *gewu* learning wrongly, run outward all day, and only manage *taking rightness by surprise*; lifelong, *acting without realizing, practicing without examining*."

— "*Loving the beautiful color, hating the foul stench* — what then?"

— "*That* is precisely *following principle*. Heaven's principle is exactly so; there is originally no fabricated like or dislike."

— "*Loving the beautiful color, hating the foul stench* — how is that not intent?"

— "It is *sincere intent*, not *selfish intent*. Sincere intent is just following Heaven's principle. Even so, one cannot put in a thread of added intent — *with anger or fondness, one does not get its uprightness*. One must be vast and impartial — and *that* is the original substance of the mind. Knowing this, one knows the centrality before stirring."

Bosheng said: "The Master said *if the weeds obstruct, principle says to remove them* — why is that still *starting from the bodily form*?"

The Master: "You must feel this in your own mind. When you go to remove the weeds, what mind is it? When Zhou Maoshu did not remove the weeds at his window, what mind was that?"

**99.** The Master said to a student: "In study, one must have a head-of-work; only then is there a place to land. Even without continuity, like a boat with a rudder, a touch reawakens it. Otherwise, even busy at study, one is just doing *taking rightness by surprise* — *acting without realizing, practicing without examining*; not the Great Root or Comprehending Way."

He said again: "When you have seen, both this saying and that one are right. If something works here but not there, you have not yet seen."

**100.** Some asked: "Studying for the sake of one's parents, one cannot avoid the burden of the official examinations."

The Master said: "If preparing for examinations on a parent's account 'burdens study,' would tilling a field to support a parent also be a 'burden'? The earlier Confucians said, *only fear that resolve is wrenched*. Only fear that the resolve to study is not deep and pressing."

**101.** Chongyi asked: "My ordinary intent is busy. With affairs, busy; without affairs, busy. Why?"

The Master said: "Heaven and earth's *qi*-engine has no breath of pause. Yet there is a master, so it is not before its time and not after, not hurried and not slow; through ten thousand changes, the master is always settled. Man receives this and lives. With master settled, he is like Heaven's revolving — busy though responding to the ten thousand changes, he is at ease, *Heaven's prince placid, the hundred limbs obeying command*. Without a master, one is just *qi* running loose — how could one not be busy?"

**102.** The Master said: "The great sickness in study is the love of fame."

Kan said: "I once thought this sickness was easing in me. Lately, examining closely, I find it has not eased at all. Surely it is not just being seen-by-others outwardly: praise stirs joy, blame stirs misery — *that* is the sickness."

The Master: "Just so. Name and substance face each other. Heart of substance heavy by one part, heart of name lighter by one part. With heart wholly substance, no heart of name remains. With heart of substance like hunger seeking food, thirst seeking drink, what leisure for love of fame?"

He said again: "*Distressed that at death one's name is not commensurate* — *ming* read in falling tone (commensurate). It is also the meaning of *Mencius*'s '*reputation exceeding the reality, the gentleman is ashamed of it*.' Substance falling short of name, alive one can mend; dead, no help. *Forty, fifty, and not heard of* — that means not knowing the Way, not the lack of reputation. Confucius said, *That is reputation, not insight*: how could he weigh men by reputation?"

**103.** Kan often regretted. The Master said: "Regret is the medicine that drives sickness, but its merit is in *changing*. If one keeps it within, the medicine itself becomes a sickness."

**104.** Dezhang said: "I have heard the Master compare the sage to pure gold, his capacity by mass, his work by smelting — most cutting. Only — Yao and Shun as ten thousand *yi*, Confucius as nine thousand — I do not feel at ease."

The Master said: "Once again, this starts from the bodily form, and so quarrels over the sage's mass. If one does not start from the bodily form, ten thousand for Yao and Shun is not too much, nine thousand for Confucius not too few. To say sage one speaks only of *discrimination and unity*, not of more and less. The point is that the mind is purely Heaven's principle — same. As for force and vigor, why must they be quite the same? Later Confucians weigh by mass, and so flow into profit. Take away the comparing-mass mind: each man fully employs his own strength and spirit, all on this mind purely Heaven's principle. Then *every man has his own*, *each is rounded*; the great achieves greatness, the small achieves smallness, no need to envy outwardly, all is sufficient. *That* is the real, settled work of *making goodness clear and making the self sincere*. Later Confucians, ignorant of the sagely learning and not knowing to embody and extend their own *innate knowing and innate ability*, go seeking what they do not know, doing what they cannot do — pining only for high and great; not knowing themselves to be Jie- or Zhou-hearted while moving to do Yao- or Shun-deeds — how could it be done? They are busy all year, even to old age and death, not knowing what they have accomplished. Pitiable indeed!"

**105.** Kan asked: "The earlier Confucian takes the *stillness* of the mind as *substance*, the *motion* of the mind as *function*. How is that?"

The Master said: "Substance and function are not motion and stillness. Motion and stillness are *time*. When speaking of substance, function is *in* substance; when speaking of function, substance is *in* function. So *substance and function one source*. To say *stillness shows substance, motion shows function* — fine."

**106.** Asked: "*The highest in wisdom and the lowest in foolishness — why unmovable?*"

The Master: "Not *unable* to move; *unwilling* to move."

**107.** Asked about *Zixia's disciples and Zizhang on friendship*.

The Master: "Zixia spoke of the friendship of small lads; Zizhang spoke of the friendship of grown men. Used well, both are right."

**108.** Ziren asked: "*Studying, and timely practicing — is this not joy?* Earlier Confucians took *study* as imitating what those before-awakened did. How is that?"

The Master said: "*Studying* is studying to remove human desire and to preserve Heaven's principle. By turning to remove human desire and preserve Heaven's principle, one corrects oneself; one consults the masters' antiquity, sets up many practices of inquiry and discernment, examination and overcoming — but all is to remove this mind's human desire and to preserve this mind's Heaven's principle. *Imitating what those before-awakened did* says only one element of study, and seems to make it an outward search. *Timely practicing*: the *sitting like a corpse* is not specifically practicing sitting — it is practicing *this mind* while sitting; *standing like a worshiper at the suburban altar* is not specifically practicing standing — it is practicing *this mind* while standing. *Joy* is the *joy* of *the heart's natural delight in principle and rightness, as the eye delights in color and the ear in sound* — but for human desire's covering. Now, with desire receding day by day, principle and rightness fitting into the mind day by day — how should there not be joy?"

**109.** Guoying asked: "Zengzi's *three-fold daily examination* was urgent, but probably the work before he had heard of *the one thread*?"

The Master said: "*One thread* came when the Master saw that Zengzi did not yet have the kernel of his work, and so told him. If the student really works on *loyalty and reciprocity*, is that not *the one thread*? *One* is like a tree's root; *thread* is the branches and leaves. Without first a root, where do branches and leaves come from? Substance and function are one source: substance unestablished, where can function come from? Saying that Zengzi had been examining each affair carefully and acting strenuously and only lacked the unity of substance — perhaps not quite right."

**110.** Huang Chengfu asked about *You and Hui — which surpasses?*

The Master said: "Zigong worked at *much study and much memory*, on the side of seen and heard; Yan Hui worked on the heart-ground. So the sage drew Zigong out by question. Zigong answered again only on the side of seen-and-heard, and so the sage sighed regretfully — not approval."

**111.** "Yan Hui's *no transferred anger, no repeated fault* is also possible only with *centrality before stirring*."

**112.** "He who plants a tree must nurture its root; he who plants virtue must cultivate his mind. Wishing the tree to grow tall, one must cut superfluous shoots at first sprout; wishing virtue to flourish, one must remove outward fondnesses at first study. Outward fondness for poetry — vital force daily leaks into poetry; the same for any outward fondness." He said again: "What I am teaching is the work of *making something out of nothing*. Trust in establishing resolve. The student's single thought of doing good is like a tree's seed: only do not 'help' and do not 'forget,' simply attend to nurturing — naturally day and night it grows; vital force fills out daily; branches and leaves prosper. When a tree is just born and puts out a busy sprout, one must trim them — only then can the trunk grow strong. So at the beginning of study. Therefore the value of resolve is *concentrated and one*."

**113.** Discussing the school: "So-and-so works on nourishment; so-and-so on insight." The Master said: "He who works only on nourishment daily sees his own deficiency; he who works only on insight daily sees his own surplus. The daily-deficient becomes daily-surplus; the daily-surplus becomes daily-deficient."

**114.** Liang Rifu asked: "*Abiding in reverence and fathoming principle* is two affairs; the Master takes them as one. How?"

The Master said: "Between Heaven and Earth there is just *this one* affair — how could there be two? If you would distinguish, the *three hundred forms of ritual, three thousand of bearing* — how could it stop at two? Tell me: *what* is *abiding in reverence*? *What* is *fathoming principle*?"

— "*Abiding in reverence* is the work of preserving and nourishing; *fathoming principle* is fathoming the principles of things and affairs."

— "Preserving — what is preserved?"

— "Preserving the Heaven's principle of this mind."

— "Then *that* is just *fathoming principle*. Tell me: how is one to fathom the principles of things and affairs?"

— "As in serving one's father, one fathoms the principle of filiality; in serving one's lord, the principle of loyalty."

— "Loyalty's and filiality's principle — is it on the lord and the father, or on one's own mind? If on one's own mind, then one is just fathoming the principle of *this mind*. Tell me, then, what is *reverence*?"

— "*Concentration on the One*."

— "What is *concentration on the One*?"

— "When reading, mind one on reading; when receiving guests, mind one on receiving guests."

— "Then drinking wine, mind one on the wine; coveting beauty, mind one on the beauty — *chasing the object*. How is that 'abiding in reverence'?"

Rifu asked.

— "*One* is Heaven's principle; *concentration on one* is the mind concentrated on Heaven's principle. If you only know 'concentration on one' without knowing that *the one* is principle, then in affairs you chase objects, in non-affairs you fix on emptiness. Only when, with affairs and without, the mind works always on Heaven's principle — *abiding in reverence* is *fathoming principle*. From the side of fathoming-principle as concentrated, call it *abiding in reverence*; from the side of abiding-in-reverence as detailed, call it *fathoming principle*. Not that one *first* abides in reverence and *then* the mind fathoms; or, in fathoming, has another mind that abides. The names differ; the work is one. As the *Changes* says, *with reverence be straight within, with rightness be square without*. *Reverence* is the *rightness* of the no-affair time; *rightness* is the *reverence* of the affair-time. The two phrases are one. Confucius's *cultivate oneself with reverence* — no need to add *rightness*. Mencius's *gather rightness* — no need to add *reverence*. Once you understand, all the said-things are the same. If one binds oneself in the literal text and misses the kernel, one fragments and the work has nowhere to land."

— "How is *fathoming principle* *fully realizing nature*?"

— "The substance of the mind is *nature*; *nature* is *principle*. Fathoming the principle of humaneness, one truly extends humaneness to its limit; fathoming the principle of rightness, one truly extends rightness to its limit. Humaneness and rightness are just my nature; therefore *fathoming principle* is *fully realizing nature*. As Mencius says, *fill out the heart of pity, and humaneness is inexhaustible* — that is the work of fathoming principle."

Rifu: "The earlier Confucians said *each blade of grass and each tree has principle, and one cannot fail to investigate*. How?"

The Master: "I have no leisure for that. You take care first to understand your own *nature and feelings*, learn to *fully realize human nature*, and only then can you *fully realize the nature of things*."

Rifu was startled into awakening.

**115.** Weiqian asked: "How is *knowing* the original substance of the mind?"

The Master said: "Knowing is the *spirit-place* of principle. In its master-aspect, call it *mind*; in its endowed-aspect, call it *nature*. The infant child who does not yet *not know* to love its parents, *not know* to revere its elder brother — only this lit lit-thing not blocked by selfish desire, fully extended, is whole. Whole is its original substance — at one with the virtue of Heaven and Earth. From sage on down, one cannot avoid cloud-cover; therefore *gewu* to extend his knowing."

**116.** Shouheng asked: "*The Great Learning*'s work is just *making intent sincere*. *Making intent sincere*'s work is just *gewu*. *Self-cultivation, family-ordering, state-governing, world-pacifying* — all in sincerity of intent. And yet there is also a *work of rectifying the mind*: *with anger or with fondness, one does not get the uprightness*. Why?"

The Master said: "Feel this out yourself. Once you know this, you know the centrality before stirring."

Shouheng asked again, three times. The Master: "The work of study has shallow and deep. At the start, without setting one's intent in earnest to love good and hate evil, how can one do good and remove evil? *That* setting-of-intent is sincerity of intent. But what is unknown is — the original substance of the mind has not a thing. With insistent intent on loving good and hating evil, one again adds an intent — and is no longer *vast and impartial*. The *Documents*'s *no fabricated likes, no fabricated dislikes* is the original substance. So we say: *with anger or with fondness, one does not get its uprightness*. *Rectifying the mind* is just within the work of *making intent sincere*, embodying one's mind-substance — always rinsed empty and balanced. *That* is the centrality before stirring."

**117.** Zhengzhi asked: "*Vigilance and apprehension* is the work where I do not yet know; *being watchful when alone* is the work where I alone know. How?"

The Master said: "It is one work. In no-affair, certainly *alone-knowing*; in affair, also *alone-knowing*. If, in this place where *I alone know*, one does not work, but only at places shared with others — *that* is fakery, *seeing the gentleman, then looking ashamed*. *This* alone-knowing is the sprout of sincerity. Here, neither good thought nor evil thought has any falseness; right at one stroke, all is right; wrong at one stroke, all is wrong — *the very boundary between sage and tyrant, righteous and profit-seeking, sincere and false, good and evil*. Standing here once, one establishes the root and clears the source — *that* is *establishing sincerity*. The ancients' work of self-sincerity, with all its spirit and pulse, lay just here. Truly *no place more visible, no thing more manifest*; no time, no place, no end, no beginning — just this one work. To call *vigilance and apprehension* a work where I do *not* know would fragment the work and leave intervals. Once vigilant, that is *knowing*. If I do not know, who is being vigilant? Such a view trickles into Buddhism's quiescence."

— "*No falseness — neither good nor evil* — is alone-knowing then *no thought*?"

— "Vigilance and apprehension are also *thought*. The thought of vigilance is never to be relaxed. With it relaxed for an instant, if not stupor, then already gone over to evil thought. From morning to night, from youth to old age — to be *without thought* is *not to know* — only stupefied or like dead wood and dead ash."

**118.** Zhidao asked: "Xunzi said *for nourishing the mind, nothing is better than sincerity*. The earlier Confucians faulted him. Why?"

The Master said: "Not necessarily wrong. *Sincerity* may be spoken of as *work*. Sincerity is the original substance of the mind. To seek to recover it is the work of *thinking sincerity*. Mingdao's *preserve it with sincerity and reverence* has the same meaning. *The Great Learning*: *to rectify the mind, first make the intent sincere*. Xunzi has many faults; one cannot pluck hairs from him as a rule. To read others, with a fixed view in mind, leads to overshooting. Mencius takes from Yang Hu the saying *being rich does not go with humaneness*; this is a sage-and-worthy's vast and public mind."

**119.** Xiao Hui asked: "My selfish thought is hard to overcome. What to do?"

The Master said: "Bring out your selfish thought; I will overcome it for you."

The Master said: "A man must have *the heart for himself* before he can overcome himself; and only by overcoming himself does he *make* himself."

Xiao Hui: "I, too, have a heart for myself. I do not know why I cannot overcome."

The Master: "Tell me how it is."

After a long while: "I, too, have a single heart wishing to be a good man, and so think I have a heart for myself. Now I see — it is only for that *bodily-shell self*, not the *true self*."

The Master: "When did the *true self* leave the bodily shell? I fear you have not even been *for the bodily-shell self*. Tell me — your so-called bodily-shell self — is it not the ear, eye, mouth, nose, and limbs?"

— "Yes. Because of this, the eye wants color, the ear sound, the mouth flavor, the limbs ease — therefore I cannot overcome."

The Master: "*Beautiful colors blind the eye; beautiful sounds deafen the ear; beautiful tastes spoil the mouth; galloping and hunting madden* — these all *harm* your ear, eye, mouth, nose, and limbs. How are they 'for' your ear, eye, mouth, nose, and limbs? If you would be *for* them, you must consider how the ear should listen, how the eye see, how the mouth speak, how the limbs move; *not seeing, hearing, speaking, moving against ritual*. Only then are the ear, eye, mouth, nose, and limbs becoming what they ought to be. Now you run all day after fame and profit — outward objects of the bodily shell. If you were truly *for* the ear and so on, then to *not see, hear, speak, or move against ritual* — would the ear, eye, mouth, nose, and limbs do this on their own? It must come from your mind. The seeing, hearing, speaking, moving are all from your mind — your mind's seeing opens through the eye; its hearing through the ear; its speaking through the mouth; its moving through the limbs. Without your mind, no ear, no eye. *Your mind* is not just that lump of flesh. If it were, the dead, with their lump of flesh still there, would be able to see, hear, speak, move. The mind is the *one able to see, hear, speak, move*; it is *nature*; it is *Heaven's principle*. With this nature there is life; this nature's principle of generation, called *humaneness*, sent forth in the eye becomes seeing, in the ear becomes hearing, in the mouth becomes speaking, in the limbs becomes moving; all is Heaven's principle issuing forth — and as it presides over the body, called *mind*. The original substance of this mind is just Heaven's principle; originally, no contrary-to-ritual; that *is* your *true self*. The true self is the master of the bodily shell. Without the true self, no bodily shell — present, alive; absent, dead. If you are truly *for* that bodily-shell self, you must use *this* true self; you must constantly preserve and protect the substance of the true self, *vigilant where one cannot see, apprehensive where one cannot hear*, fearing only to harm it by a hair. The instant a thread of contrary-to-ritual sprouts, feel it like a knife's cut, like a needle's prick, unbearable — until you have removed the knife and pulled the needle: *that* is *for one's own self*; only this can overcome. Now you take the thief for the son and yet say you have a heart for yourself but cannot overcome — how can it be?"

**120.** A student suffering from eye trouble was deeply distressed. The Master: "You value the eye and devalue the mind."

**121.** Xiao Hui was fond of Daoism and Buddhism. The Master warned him: "I, too, gave my youth to the Two Schools, thought I had gained from them, took the Confucian as not worth study. Three years among the southern peoples I saw that the sage's learning is so simple, so vast, and at last I sighed in regret at having wasted thirty years' strength. Daoism and Buddhism, in their subtleties, differ from the sage by hairs. What you study is their offal — yet you confide in it like this. Truly, an owl pecking at a putrid mouse!"

Xiao Hui asked the Master to set out the Two Schools' subtleties.

The Master: "I have already told you, the sage's learning is simple and vast. You did not ask what I have *seen*, but what I have *regretted*."

Hui apologized and asked the sage's learning.

The Master: "I have already told you in a sentence; you still do not get it."

**122.** Liu Guanshi asked: "What is the centrality before stirring?"

The Master: "Be vigilant where you cannot see, apprehensive where you cannot hear; nourish this mind till it is purely Heaven's principle, and you will see of yourself."

Guanshi asked for some hint of the air.

The Master: "*A mute eats the bitter melon; he cannot tell you. To know the bitterness, eat it yourself.*"

Yueren beside the Master said: "*That* is real knowing — that *is* acting." All present that day had insight.

**123.** Xiao Hui asked about *the Way of life and death*.

The Master: "Know day and night, and you know life and death."

— "What of *the Way of day and night*?"

— "Know the day, and you know the night."

— "Is there even a knowing of *day*?"

— "You know *day*? — to rise muddled, to eat dazed, *to act without realizing, to practice without examining*, all-day stupor: *that* is dreaming the day. Only with breath nourished, with vigilance present, this mind alert and bright, Heaven's principle without an instant of break — *that* is being able to know the day. *That* is Heaven's virtue; *that* is *passing through the Way of day and night and knowing*; what death and life are there to add?"

**124.** Ma Zixin asked: "*The teaching of cultivating the Way* — the old reading: *the sage tempers and articulates what our nature originally has, to be a model under Heaven, like ritual, music, punishment, and government*. How?"

The Master said: "The Way *is* nature *is* mandate. Originally complete, neither addable nor subtractable, requiring no decoration. Why need the sage to temper and articulate? — that would make it incomplete. Ritual, music, punishment, government are means of governing the world; they may be called *teaching*, but not Zisi's original meaning. If, as the earlier Confucians have it, one *enters the Way through teaching*, and the entry is the sage's ritual, music, punishment, and government — why does the *Doctrine of the Mean* immediately set out a separate work of *vigilance and apprehension*? It would make the sage's teaching empty trim. Zisi's *nature, Way, teaching* all spoke from the root. *Heaven's mandate*, given to man, is called *nature*; *following nature* in act, then nature is the *Way*; *cultivating the Way* in study, then the Way is the *teaching*. *Following nature* is the work of *the sincere*; therefore *from sincerity comes brightness, called nature*. *Cultivating the Way* is the work of *being-sincere*; therefore *from brightness comes sincerity, called teaching*. The sage *follows nature in act*, just *is* the Way. Below the sage, none follows nature; one cannot avoid excess and deficiency, and so must *cultivate the Way*. Cultivating it, the wise and worthy cannot exceed, the foolish and unworthy cannot fall short — all must follow this Way, and the Way *becomes* the teaching. This *teaching* is the same as *Heaven's Way is the perfect teaching; rain, dew, frost are nothing but teaching*; and *cultivate the Way* is the same as *cultivate the Way with humaneness*. Once man can cultivate the Way, he no longer departs from it; his nature is restored to its substance — that, too, is the sage's Way. The next words — *vigilant and apprehensive* — are the work of *cultivating the Way*; *centrality and harmony* are the restoring of nature's substance. As the *Changes* says, *fathoming principle, fully realizing nature, arriving at the mandate*; *centrality and harmony, the placing-in-rank and the nourishing* — *that* is *fully realizing nature, arriving at the mandate*."

**125.** Huang Chengfu asked: "The earlier Confucians take Confucius's 'On Yan Yuan's question on governing the state' to be *the constant Way of myriad ages*. How?"

The Master said: "Yan Hui had the substance of the sage; on the great roots and great sources of governing the state he was already complete. The Master in his ordinary days had read him deeply; here he need not say it, only setting out matters of system and form. These too must not be neglected; one must do them this way fully. Nor must one, on the strength of one's foundations, be lax about safeguards: one must *banish the music of Zheng and reach the flatterer*. Yan Hui was a man who *overcame the self* and *worked on virtue inwardly*. Confucius feared he would be loose on the outward branches, and so filled in for him. To another man, Confucius would have had to set out: *governing lies in men; choose men by self; cultivate self by Way; cultivate the Way with humaneness*; and the *nine standards of the comprehensive Way*; and the *self-sincerity* work — only then could the work get done; *that* would be the constant Way of myriad ages. Otherwise, merely follow the season of Xia, ride the carriage of Yin, wear the Zhou cap, dance the Shao — and the world is governed? Later men, seeing Yan Hui as the foremost of Confucius's school and his question on *governing the state*, took it for the great affair of the world."

**126.** Cai Xiyuan asked: "Master Wenzhong's new edition of the *Great Learning* puts *gewu* and *zhizhi* before *making intent sincere*, which seems to fit the sequence of the opening chapter. The Master, holding to the old text, has *making intent sincere* before *gewu zhizhi*. I am still not at peace."

The Master said: "The work of the *Great Learning* *is* illuminating bright virtue; illuminating bright virtue *is* making intent sincere; the work of making intent sincere *is* gewu *and* zhizhi. Take *making intent sincere* as the master, and apply *gewu zhizhi* — only then does the work have a place to land; doing good and removing evil are in every case the affair of making intent sincere. As in the new edition, where one first goes to fathom the principles of things, one drifts in vague waste, with no place to land; one must add the word *reverence* to drag it back to *body and mind* — and even so, no source. Why need to add *reverence*? Why would Confucius drop the most urgent character and leave it to be added a thousand years later? Just because *making intent sincere* is master, no need to add *reverence*. So the *making intent sincere* is set forth — the *great head-of-work* of study. Miss this and you have *the difference of hairs and miles*. The *Doctrine of the Mean*'s work is *making the body sincere*; its peak is *utmost sincerity*. The *Great Learning*'s work is *making intent sincere*; its peak is *the supreme good*. The work is one. To say here *add reverence*, there *add sincerity*, is painting feet on a snake."

— *End of Chapter 1.*
